Dashboard Templates

Users were having difficulty setting up dashboard views in LiquidPlanner's project management software. I helped research and design a new way for users to get value from this key product feature.

Overview

  • Company LiquidPlanner
  • Timeline 2 months in early 2018
  • Methods Interaction Design, User Research, Usability Testing
  • Team Edward Nguyen, Leanna Gingras, Michael Ortlieb, Nick Smith, Kayvon Ghaffari

The Problem LiquidPlanner provides collaborative project management software that helps teams plan and execute work. Dashboards, a feature that helps teams set up a hub to collaborate and visualize their work, were difficult to set up. LiquidPlanner’s Support team communicated this pain to Product and that most users struggled to use and get value from the feature.

Solution We researched and designed pre-built dashboard templates that supports LiquidPlanner’s competitiveness in the market, while making it easy for users to create valuable visualizations and dashboards in just a few clicks.

Our Approach

  1. Research: conduct customer research and analyze market competition
  2. Define: the problems to be solved and insights around areas of opportunity
  3. Design: ideate possible solutions, prototyped and tested with users
  4. Evaluation: usability tests to get feedback
  5. Refine: iterated based on learnings from testing
  6. Post-ship: monitored adoption and success of design, share with org

My Contribution During the research phase, I conducted interviews with users of dashboards and customer success to learn about user frustrations and needs. My team then synthesized the interviews and generated a set of insights. I created user flow diagrams and storyboards to illustrate the user’s experience and gain shared empathy within and outside our team.

I then co-led a design session to generate possible solutions. Once a direction was formed, I created an interactive prototype to test with users and took the lead of recruiting and running usability tests with seven customers. Findings from testing were then incorporated into a refined, final design. Lastly, I supported the engineering team with spec documents to implement the new feature.

Design Outcome

Research

Understanding the user through interviews

Our team learned about dashboard issues months before the project was taken on. My first task was to learn directly from users and customer success, who channeled many of the repeated pain points and frustrations they encountered when working with customers. My main goal was to learn what problems users were having and how they expected dashboards to work for them.

A big pattern and point of frustration coming from interviews was the pain around filtering. In LiquidPlanner, dashboards are comprised of many “widgets” or dashboard tiles. In order for widgets to display information, a filter must be used to specify what content to show. Users had great difficulty using the filters and it was a major stumbling point. Our Product Marketing Manager told me, “if a customer doesn’t understand how to use filters, they can’t use Dashboards”. This was primarily a problem when creating new dashboards and was a barrier to entry. In the example below, a donut chart widget is being configured - clicking the filter icon brings up a dropdown shown right.

Examining dashboard use

In addition to interviews, our team wanted to learn what users were ultimately after. What was their goal with using dashboards? To solve this we conducted a content inventory of hundreds of dashboards, sourced from customers who opted to share their dashboards with the team. We found many commonalities around types of dashboards, projects, teams, and portfolios. Many shared similar widget types and displayed data.

Define

Insights

Through affinity mapping we synthesized our results and gained the following insights and defined user needs:

  • The moment of creating a dashboard was the main source of pain, as any new dashboard had to be made from scratch / a blank slate. It was difficult to have to create so many widgets by-hand.
  • Creating widgets is difficult because of filters, which are difficult to understand and use properly and is the primary blocker of dashboard set up.
  • Competitive PM products made dashboard creation much easier for their users, as users never had to start from a blank slate.
  • The issues above primarily affects project and team managers, the personas most commonly using dashboards. The difficulty of dashboards made them spend much more time than they wanted to in LiquidPlanner.
  • Users used dashboards in very similar ways, with many similarities with widget types.

I created a storyboard that could be shared internally to get reactions to the problem and begin early ideation on potential solutions. Current user flow

In addition, I created a flow diagram to help visualize the user’s task and primary point of frustration. I wanted to challenge myself to think of new ways to get the user to their end goal. This process is the same for any type of dashboard created.

Focusing on the Problem

While fixing filtering seemed to be the obvious solution, our team was challenged to see if there were other ways to help users. Filtering was a UI component used in many places of the product outside of dashboards, and was a big risk for scope creep. Still, our team was focused on helping users create valuable dashboards for their projects and teams, and to see if there were ways to do so without requiring fixing filters.

Ideation

Our next step was to begin design ideation. I co-led a brainstorm session and included storyboards and task flows so that participants could understand the current user experience and the problem we were trying to solve. Members from Sales, CS, and Engineering were involved.

The session led to many possible solutions, including a redesign of filters. Perhaps the most common idea that emerged was pre-built dashboards, or templates. The team honed in on this idea as we felt it solved the problem of dashboard creation best and competitors had a similar approach. In the bottom-left most sketch in the image below, we also imagined making dashboard creation simple from outside dashboards, such as within a “project page”. Notice in (3) the lack of filtering mechanism seen in earlier product screenshots. For each template type, we decided to use widgets and configurations commonly found from our content inventory.

Our proposed flow felt more straight forward. But of course, we needed to test this flow and the dashboard contents with users. The next step was beginning the recruiting process and prototyping the flow and screens.

Evaluation

User Testing

I utilized UXPin to create an interactive prototype that could be tested with customers. I used ethn.io to recruit current customers who had no prior experience using dashboards.

Results

With testing we wanted to learn if users understood the benefit of templates and if they were able to successfully complete the task of creating different dashboard types. Users were able to successfully do this across the board, and noted the simplicity of creating dashboards this way, which they enjoyed. Most of the critical feedback was around the widgets used in each template.

Widget feedback

While the base dashboards were very useful and a step-up from the existing experience, some participants were very particular about the widgets they wanted - and wanted the freedom to customize the templates further to include their own widgets. This feedback was eventually incorporated into the final design, allowing users to add their own widgets into templates. Further, we iterated on our modal screen to condense two steps into one (choose type and configure dashboard), to make the modal more comprehensive and less step oriented.

Impact

Two quarters later, our engineering team ran a query to see how users were adopting the new feature. To our surprise, the adoption rate was great. Of all dashboards created by customers, over 60% of them utilized one of the templates as part of this feature. Moreover, we heard great feedback from customers who felt it was much easier to get going with dashboards - and our customer success team noted the impact in the form of less how-to tickets and calls around dashboard set up.
