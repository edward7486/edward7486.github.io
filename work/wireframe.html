Background Information

Vertafore was releasing an enterprise application for tablet devices. They looked to me to ideate, wireframe, and prototype for an alert panel (bell icon that notifies of events/attention items). To start, we began by forming an understanding of how alerts function as a series of three steps:

  • Aware: how do users become aware of an alert?
  • Consume: how do they read or consume an alert?
  • Administer: how can they configure alerts?

A Practice of Asking for Feedback

My first step in the process was to immediately get my ideas out on paper. I sketched a ton and constantly got feedback from peers in my design team, but also externally with developers and others. I wanted to design something that was familiar in terms of design, layout, and interaction. Touching base with normal everyday users in the company was a great way to validate my designs moving forward, and to design something that made sense.

Each designer presented their ideas and designs at an ideation session as a team. In the end, a blend of many ideas factored into the final design pattern.

Alert History and Settings

Next, I was responsible for handling the archived view of alerts, the alert history, and the alert settings/configuration. I practiced the same approach of rapidly designing so I could show early and often, getting quick feedback. I collaborated heavily with a peer in the design team. My usual process was showing two versions of essentially the same feature, and discussing and brainstorming which approach works, which doesn't, and improvements and/or changes.

After gathering feedback and discussing alternatives, the consensus was that checkboxes are good for settings, and that they should be visually connected. Modals were used to stay consistent with Vertafore's platform, which uses modals quite often. For alert history, it was to be modeled after the alert panel itself, but with search functionality and accordions for collapsing product groups. With the interaction patterns down, it was off to a final high-fidelity prototype.
